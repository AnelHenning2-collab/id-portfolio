What's a Control?

A control is just a safeguard — a rule or process that prevents or catches a problem.

Example: when someone leaves the company, IT is supposed to shut off their system access within 24 hours. That rule is a control.

The Audit Cycle

Auditing that control means walking through 4 steps. Tap each to see what happens, using our example.

1. Control Identified
"Terminated employees lose access within 24 hours" is written down as a control to test.
2. Evidence Requested
Ask IT for a system log showing exactly when access was removed for recent terminations.
3. Evidence Reviewed
Compare the termination date to the access-removal timestamp for each case.
4. Finding Documented
Write down whether the control worked every time, or note the cases where it didn't.

Tap all four steps to continue.

Findings vs. Recommendations

Finding
What the evidence actually showed. Example: "3 of 20 terminated employees kept access for more than 24 hours."
Recommendation
What you suggest to fix it. Example: "Automate access removal instead of relying on a manual step."

They're not the same thing — a finding is a fact, a recommendation is a suggestion.
